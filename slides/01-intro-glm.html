‚ÄúThe distribution of the errors‚Äù and ‚Äúthe distribution of the data given x‚Äù are two equivalent ways of expressing the same modeling assumption.

Normal distribution

  • Support: ‚àí‚àû,+‚àû-\infty, +\infty
  • Mean (expected value): Œº\mu
  • Variance: œÉ2\sigma^2
  • Independence: changing the mean does not change the variance!

Why does it matter?

Real-world measurements are not automatically Normal, Normality is a modeling choice.

  • Support: what range of values is possible
  • Mean (expected value): what value to predict on average
  • Variance: how much variation around the mean
  • Mean‚Äìvariance relationship: does variance change with the mean?

Probability Distributions and Random Variables

Why probability distributions?

Our models do not fit our data exactly ‚Ä¶

  1. Predict the average value of the outcome given the predictors
  2. Summarize the variation in this prediction

Probability distributions help us characterize the variation that remains after predicting the average.

‚Üí\rightarrow Quantify uncertainty in the estimated parameters of the model!

Probability Distributions

A probability distribution describes all possible values a random variable YY can take and their associated probabilities, given certain parameters.


f(y‚à£ùõâ)f(y \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})


Where:

  • YY is the random variable (uppercase)
  • yy is a specific value it can take (lowercase)
  • ùõâ\boldsymbol{\theta} is a vector of parameters (e.g., mean, variance)
  • f(‚ãÖ)f(\cdot) is the probability function

Discrete vs. Continuous Variables

Discrete random variables (counts, binary outcomes, etc.)

  • Probability Mass Function (PMF)
  • P(Y=y‚à£ùõâ)P(Y = y \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})
  • The probability of observing exactly yy
  • P(Y=3)=0.25P(Y = 3) = 0.25 means 25% chance of getting 3

Discrete vs. Continuous Variables

Continuous random variables (time, weight, temperature, etc.)

  • Probability Density Function (PDF)
  • f(y‚à£ùõâ)f(y \mid \boldsymbol{\theta})
  • The probability density at yy (not a probability!)
  • P(Y=y)=0P(Y = y) = 0 for any specific value

Distributions and linear regression

Predict the average value of YY and quantify the remaining variation:

  • Expected value (mean): E[Y]=ŒºE[Y]=\mu.
    The long-run average of YY under its distribution.

  • Conditional expected value: E[Y‚à£X]=Œº(X)E[Y\mid X]=\mu(X).
    The long-run average outcome for a fixed predictor value X=xX=x.

  • Variance: Var(Y)\mathrm{Var}(Y) (or, in regression, Var(Y‚à£X)\mathrm{Var}(Y\mid X)).
    How much outcomes vary around the mean (overall or at a given xx).

In normal linear and generalized linear models we generally include predictors on the mean of the distribution.

Why is it important?

The definitions of expected value and variance are universal, but how we compute them (and what they equal) depends on the distribution‚Äôs parameters.

For a Normal distribution Y‚àºùí©(Œº,œÉ2)Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2), the parameters are exactly the mean and variance.

  • Expected value (mean): E[Y]=ŒºE[Y]=\mu.
  • Variance: Var(Y)=œÉ2\mathrm{Var}(Y)=\sigma^2.

Generalized Linear Models (GLM)

General ideas

  • using distributions beyond the Normal
  • modeling non linear functions on the response scale
  • taking into account mean-variance relationships

The three ingredients of a GLM

  1. Random Component: Choose a distribution

  2. Systematic Component: Linear predictor Œ∑i=Œ≤0+Œ≤1xi\eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i

  3. Link Function: Transform the mean g(Œºi)=Œ∑ig(\mu_i) = \eta_i

Random component

The random component specifies a probability model for yiy_i:


yi‚à£xi‚àºDistribution(parameters). y_i \mid x_i \sim \text{Distribution}(\text{parameters}).


What support?

  • Binary: {0,1}\{0,1\} ‚Üí\rightarrow Bernoulli(p)\text{Bernoulli}(p) (or Binomial(1,p)\text{Binomial}(1,p))
  • Counts: {0,1,2,‚Ä¶}\{0,1,2,\ldots\} ‚Üí\rightarrow Poisson(Œª)\text{Poisson}(\lambda)
  • Any real number: (‚àí‚àû,‚àû)(-\infty,\infty) ‚Üí\rightarrow Normal(Œº,œÉ2)\text{Normal}(\mu,\sigma^2)

Random component (mean‚Äìvariance)

Choosing a distribution specifies not only the mean, but also how the variance depends on the mean:

Var(Y‚à£X)=œïV(Œº),Œº=E(Y‚à£X), \mathrm{Var}(Y \mid X) = \phi \, V(\mu), \qquad \mu = E(Y \mid X),

where V(Œº)V(\mu) is the variance function (determined by the family) and œï\phi is the dispersion parameter.

  • Normal: V(Œº)=1V(\mu) = 1 (constant variance, œï=œÉ2\phi = \sigma^2)
  • Bernoulli: V(Œº)=Œº(1‚àíŒº)V(\mu) = \mu(1-\mu) (typically œï=1\phi = 1)
  • Poisson: V(Œº)=ŒºV(\mu) = \mu (typically œï=1\phi = 1)

Note

If œï>1\phi>1 but you fit a Poisson/Bernoulli model with œï=1\phi=1, the coefficient estimates Œ≤ÃÇ\hat\beta may look similar, but the standard errors are smaller.

Systematic Component

The systematic component is exactly the same as in normal linear regression: we predict a linear combination of predictors.


Œ∑i=Œ≤0+Œ≤1xi1+Œ≤2xi2+‚ãØ+Œ≤kxik. \eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_{i1} + \beta_2 x_{i2} + \cdots + \beta_k x_{ik}.

Basically it describes how the expected value (i.e., the mean) of the chosen distribution (the random component) varies according to the predictors.

Link Function

The link function g(‚ãÖ)g(\cdot) connects the expected value (mean) Œºi\mu_i of the distribution to the linear predictor Œ∑i\eta_i:


g(Œºi)=Œ∑i g(\mu_i) = \eta_i


  • The linear predictor Œ∑i\eta_i can be any real number: (‚àí‚àû,+‚àû)(-\infty, +\infty)
  • But Œºi\mu_i (the mean) is constrained by the distribution‚Äôs support
  • The link function transforms Œºi\mu_i to be unbounded

  • Counts of successes out of nn trials (e.g., items correct out of nn).

Why not model probability directly?


Problem for binary outcomes: A linear model can predict values outside [0,1], but probabilities must stay between 0 and 1.


Solution: Transform the probability to a scale that ranges from ‚àí‚àû-\infty to +‚àû+\infty, then model that with a linear predictor.

Odds

The odds compare the probability of success to the probability of failure:

odds=p1‚àíp,p=oddsodds+1 \text{odds} = \frac{p}{1-p}, \qquad p = \frac{\text{odds}}{\text{odds}+1}


Example: If p=0.80p=0.80 (80% chance of passing), then

odds=0.800.20=4. \text{odds}=\frac{0.80}{0.20}=4.


We say the odds are 4-to-1 in favor of passing (expect 4 passes for every 1 failure, on average).


Range: Odds go from 0 to +‚àû+\infty, but still can‚Äôt be negative.

Logit link

Taking the logarithm of the odds gives us a scale from ‚àí‚àû-\infty to +‚àû+\infty:

Inverse logit link

logit(p)=log(p1‚àíp)=Œ∑,p=exp(Œ∑)1+exp(Œ∑). \text{logit}(p)=\log\!\left(\frac{p}{1-p}\right)=\eta, \qquad p = \frac{\exp(\eta)}{1+\exp(\eta)}.

This is what we model linearly: Œ∑=Œ≤0+Œ≤1x\eta = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x.

Example

Let Yi‚àà{0,1}Y_i \in \{0,1\} (Pass), and pi=P(Yi=1‚à£xi)p_i = P(Y_i=1 \mid x_i) where xix_i = hours studied.


Odds: oi=pi1‚àípio_i = \dfrac{p_i}{1-p_i} (how much more likely pass than fail).



Log-odds (logit): logit(pi)=log(pi1‚àípi)\text{logit}(p_i)=\log\!\left(\dfrac{p_i}{1-p_i}\right).




Model: logit(pi)=Œ∑i,Œ∑i=Œ≤0+Œ≤1xi. \text{logit}(p_i) = \eta_i,\quad \eta_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i .


We model the log-odds as linear in xx. What does this mean for the odds and probabilities?

Odds ratios

Our model: logit(pi)=Œ≤0+Œ≤1xi\text{logit}(p_i) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i


Exponentiating both sides gives the odds:


o(x)=p(x)1‚àíp(x)=exp(Œ≤0+Œ≤1x). o(x) = \frac{p(x)}{1-p(x)} = \exp(\beta_0 + \beta_1 x).


At x=0x=0: o(0)=exp(Œ≤0)o(0)=\exp(\beta_0).


At x=1x=1: o(1)=exp(Œ≤0+Œ≤1)=exp(Œ≤0)exp(Œ≤1)o(1)=\exp(\beta_0+\beta_1)=\exp(\beta_0)\exp(\beta_1).


Odds ratios

Odds ratio for +1 hour:


o(1)o(0)=exp(Œ≤0)exp(Œ≤1)exp(Œ≤0)=exp(Œ≤1). \frac{o(1)}{o(0)}=\frac{\exp(\beta_0)\exp(\beta_1)}{\exp(\beta_0)} =\exp(\beta_1).


so exp(Œ≤1)\exp(\beta_1) is the multiplicative change in odds for each additional study hour.

Linear on log-odds

Equal changes in xx ‚Üí\rightarrow equal changes in Œ∑\eta (because Œ∑\eta is linear in xx), but not in odds. Each +1 increase in xx multiplies the odds by exp(Œ≤1)\exp(\beta_1).

Not linear in probability

Equal changes in xx generally do not correspond to equal changes in pp.
